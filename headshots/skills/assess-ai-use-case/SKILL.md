Assess AI Use Case
When to use
Use when Paula needs to assess a new AI, automation, or Copilot use case entering the intake process.
Use when Paula asks whether an AI idea is worth pursuing, pausing, rejecting, or refining.
Use when Paula needs a clear recommendation for prioritisation, governance, or a steering group.
Use when a stakeholder request sounds exciting but the business value, feasibility, risk, or adoption need is unclear.
Do not use when Paula is asking for a delivery plan for an already approved use case, use the AI Delivery Lead instead.
Inputs needed
Use case description, required. If missing, ask: "What is the AI use case or Copilot idea you want assessed?"
Business problem or opportunity, required. If missing, ask: "What problem, inefficiency, risk, or opportunity is this meant to address?"
Expected outcome or benefit, required. If missing, ask: "What measurable result would make this successful?"
Stakeholders and owner, required. If missing, ask: "Who owns the problem, who will use the solution, and who would own the outcome?"
Known constraints, optional. If missing, ask only if needed: "Are there any known data, security, technology, process, adoption, or governance constraints?"
Steps
Restate the use case in plain English, focused on the business problem rather than the technology.
Identify the expected value, including measurable benefits, qualitative benefits, and who benefits.
Assess feasibility across data, technology, process maturity, stakeholder readiness, and delivery effort.
Assess risks, including security, privacy, governance, adoption, reputational, operational, and value-realisation risks.
Identify dependencies, blockers, assumptions, and unanswered questions.
Score or classify the use case using value, feasibility, risk, adoption complexity, and strategic alignment.
Give a clear recommendation: Proceed, Proceed with conditions, Reassess later, or Do not proceed.
Output contract
Keep the output concise, structured, and suitable for Paula to use in an intake discussion, prioritisation forum, or governance update.
Use headings, short bullets, and one summary table.
Include business problem, expected benefits, feasibility, risks, dependencies, prioritisation view, recommendation, and next steps.
Do not overpromise AI benefits, assume value without evidence, or recommend delivery without naming risks and conditions.
Do not use technical jargon unless the user provided it or it is necessary for the assessment.

One worked example
Inputs:
Use case: Use Copilot to summarise recurring project meetings and extract actions.
Business problem: Project teams spend too much time writing notes and actions are inconsistently tracked.
Expected outcome: Reduce meeting admin time and improve action follow-up.
Stakeholders: Project managers, Business Analysts, delivery teams.
Constraints: Needs guidance on responsible use and storage of meeting content.
Output:
Recommended decision: Proceed with conditions.
Rationale: Clear productivity benefit, strong fit for Copilot, moderate adoption complexity, and manageable governance risk if guidance is provided.
Key conditions: Define meeting types in scope, agree action-tracking process, provide responsible-use guidance, and measure time saved.
Next steps: Pilot with three project teams, capture baseline admin time, create a simple user guide, and review results after four weeks.
Test it
Assess this AI use case: a business team wants to use Copilot to summarise customer feedback emails, identify recurring themes, and create a weekly insight report for managers.
